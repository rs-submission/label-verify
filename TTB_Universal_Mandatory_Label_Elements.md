# TTB Universal Mandatory Label Elements — Reference

The label elements required on **every** TTB-regulated alcohol beverage
(distilled spirits, wine, and malt beverages). Each item below applies across all
three commodities; the governing regulation differs by commodity (Part 4 = wine,
Part 5 = distilled spirits, Part 7 = malt beverages), with the Government Health
Warning governed for all by Part 16.

*Working reference, not legal advice. Confirm against the current eCFR and the
applicable Beverage Alcohol Manual (BAM) before filing a COLA. Commodity-specific
and conditional items (e.g., sulfite declaration, FD&C Yellow #5, appellation of
origin) are covered separately — see the companion class-and-type reference.*

---

## The mandatory elements

| # | Element | What it is | Governing rule |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Brand name** | The name under which the product is marketed. Must be truthful and not misleading. If there is no brand name, the producer's name serves as it. | Part 4 / Part 5 / Part 7 |
| 2 | **Class / type designation** | The standard-of-identity designation (e.g., "Vodka," "Red Wine," "Pilsner") or, where none applies, a statement of composition with a fanciful name. | 27 CFR 4.34; 5.63; 7.63 |
| 3 | **Alcohol content** | Stated as "Alcohol __% by Volume" (or ABV), subject to the exceptions below. | Part 4 / Part 5 / Part 7 |
| 4 | **Net contents** | The volume of beverage in the container (e.g., 750 mL, 12 fl oz). | Part 4 / Part 5 / Part 7 |
| 5 | **Name and address** | Of the bottler, packer, producer, or importer, matching the federal basic permit (city and state). | Part 4 / Part 5 / Part 7 |
| 6 | **Country of origin** | Required on imported products. | CBP rule, 19 CFR Part 134 |
| 7 | **Government Health Warning Statement** | The verbatim statutory warning (see below). Required on any beverage ≥ 0.5% ABV. | 27 CFR Part 16 |

---

## The "same field of vision" rule

Three of the elements above — the **brand name**, **alcohol content**, and
**class/type designation** — must appear together in the **same field of vision**:
a single side of the container that can be read at once without turning it. This
grouping is the "brand label."

The remaining mandatory items (net contents, name and address, country of origin,
and the health warning) may appear on **any** label on the container.

---

## Government Health Warning Statement

Required on every alcohol beverage at **0.5% ABV or higher**. It must be reproduced
**word-for-word**, with **"GOVERNMENT WARNING"** in **bold capital letters**, and
must appear separate and apart from other information. The mandated text is:

> **GOVERNMENT WARNING:** (1) According to the Surgeon General, women should not
> drink alcoholic beverages during pregnancy because of the risk of birth defects.
> (2) Consumption of alcoholic beverages impairs your ability to drive a car or
> operate machinery, and may cause health problems.

A single deviation — a missing comma, wrong capitalization, or un-bolded heading —
is grounds for COLA rejection. Minimum type-size requirements apply and scale with
container size.

---

## Alcohol content — the exceptions

The numerical alcohol statement is mandatory **except**:

- **Wine, 7–14% ABV:** the class designation **"table wine"** or **"light wine"**
  may be used on the brand label *in lieu of* a numerical alcohol statement.
- **Malt beverages / beer:** alcohol content is **not currently federally mandatory**
  for many malt beverages (it is permitted, and some **states** require it).

> **Watch this item.** TTB's proposed rule **Notice No. 237** ("Alcohol Facts"
> statements) would, if finalized, require per-serving alcohol, calorie, and
> nutrient disclosure on all classes of alcohol beverage labels, and would impose
> mandatory alcohol content statements on certain malt beverages, beer, and wine
> not currently required to carry one. As of mid-2026 this is a **proposal, not yet
> in effect** — but it is the element most likely to change this list.

---

## Sub-0.5% ABV products

Beverages under 0.5% ABV are not "alcohol beverages" for federal excise-tax purposes
and are **not** required to carry the Government Health Warning Statement. Malt-based
versions can still fall under the Federal Alcohol Administration (FAA) Act, and
naming is restricted (e.g., "non-alcoholic" requires a qualifying statement;
"alcohol free" is reserved for 0.0% ABV and requires formula/lab verification).

---

## Underlying authorization

Before any product enters U.S. commerce, its label must hold a **Certificate of
Label Approval (COLA)** or a COLA exemption from TTB.
